Task 1: Digital Literacy Awareness Infographic
Tool Used: Canva
Description: For this task, I designed a single-page visual resource aimed at first-year students. The design features a clean, three-section layout. The first section defines Digital Literacy as the ability to find, evaluate, and communicate information via typing and digital media. The middle section highlights Essential Digital Tools, specifically mentioning Notion for note-taking and Zotero for citations. The final section focuses on Email Etiquette, providing a "Golden Rules" list such as using clear subject lines and professional sign-offs.

Reflection:
What I found most interesting was the challenge of "visual hierarchy"—deciding which information deserved the biggest font to grab a student's attention quickly. It was difficult to condense complex topics like safe internet practices into just a few bullet points without losing the core message, but it taught me the value of concise communication.

Task 4: Professional Email & Etiquette Guide
Reflection on Communication:
A situation where poor communication caused a problem is when a student sent a WhatsApp message to a professor at 11:00 PM using slang (e.g., "Hey Prof, can't send the work, net is down, thx"). The professor found it disrespectful and did not grant the extension.

What could be done differently:
The student should have used a formal email sent during working hours. By using a clear subject line like "Extension Request: a polite tone explaining the technical issue, the student would have demonstrated professional maturity and likely received a more favorable response.

Task 5: Cybercrime Awareness Case Study
Reflection on Research:
While researching UPI/Online Payment Fraud, I was most surprised by how sophisticated "Screen Sharing" scams have become. Attackers often pose as customer service agents and trick users into downloading apps like AnyDesk to "fix" a refund issue, only to drain their bank accounts.

Personal Change:
As a result of this research, I have committed to a new habit: Never clicking on a "Payment Link" to receive money. I now understand that receiving money via UPI never requires entering a PIN. This awareness has made me much more cautious about unsolicited calls from "bank representatives."
